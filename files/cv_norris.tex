	% \noindent \textbf{Peers, Parents, and Attitudes about School} (Job Market Paper)
	% 	\smallskip

	% 	\noindent Abstract: Educational attitudes are an important component of adolescent development linked to long-term educational success and as a component of noncognitive skills. This study focuses on peer and parent roles in shaping adolescent attitude development. First, I explore the relationship between an adolescent and their friends' attitudes and whether this influence is heterogeneous. Second, I ask whether parents can moderate the friend effect. I find that adolescents with poor attitudes and whose friends have particularly poor attitudes are especially at risk of developing low educational attitudes and that working with parents can serve as a channel to decrease the risk.

	% \noindent \textbf{Troubled in School? Does Parental Communication Matter for Adolescents?} (with Martijn van Hasselt) 
	% 	\smallskip 

	% 	\noindent Abstract: We study the causal effect of communication between parents and adolescents on the trouble an adolescent experiences in school. Using Add Health data, we construct a measure of school trouble as a component of noncognitive skills. We use latent variable analysis to construct the composite from observed variables, such as truancy, fighting, and trouble with teachers. Conditional on test scores, higher levels of school trouble are associated with lower future wages, a finding consistent with previous literature on the returns to noncognitive skills. Using an instrumental variables strategy, we find that parent-adolescent communication reduces trouble in school. There is little evidence of unobserved heterogeneity in the communication treatment effect. However, our findings do suggest that schools may play a role in enhancing or diminishing the effect of communication with parents.

	% \noindent \textbf{The Identity Economics, Skill Development, and Peer Effects Literature: Deriving New Research Directions from a Synthesis}
	% 	\smallskip

	% 	\noindent Abstract: Within the economic literature, studies in identity economics, skill development, and peer effects have all suggested that social influences and constraints have an important role in determining choices. In this review, I draw from the literature in each of these fields to frame research gaps and questions. I focus on the interaction between family and peer effects that spread influence over networks, whether peer effects last and channels through which they do, and how identity theory informs when strong peer effects may be generated. A richer framework including more nuanced social influences and the malleability of skills offers new research opportunities and will better our understanding of policy initiatives.
